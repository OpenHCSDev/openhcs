\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

\subsection{The Central Discovery}

Five independent scientific frameworks each characterize the same architectural property.

\begin{theorem}[Five-Way Equivalence]\label{thm:five-way}
For any architecture $A$ with $\mathrm{Cap}(A) > 0$, the following are equivalent:
\[
\underbrace{\mathrm{DOF}(A) = 1}_{\text{single source}}
\;\iff\;
\underbrace{L(A) \text{ is maximal}}_{\text{engineering}}
\;\iff\;
\underbrace{\mathrm{SSOT}(A)}_{\text{epistemics}}
\;\iff\;
\underbrace{\mathrm{srank}(A) = 1}_{\text{information}}
\;\iff\;
\underbrace{\text{tractable sufficiency}}_{\text{complexity}}
\;\iff\;
\underbrace{\text{zero thermodynamic cost}}_{\text{physics}}
\]
\end{theorem}

This is proved in Section~\ref{five-way-equivalence} and machine-checked in Lean~4 via live cross-paper imports (Paper~3 $\to$ Paper~4 $\to$ Mathlib).

\textbf{Why this is surprising.} Each framework was developed independently for a different purpose: leverage for architectural optimization, SSOT for epistemic coherence, structural rank for decision theory, tractability for computational complexity, thermodynamic cost for statistical physics. They are logically independent---knowing one gives no \emph{a priori} reason to expect the others. Their convergence on a single condition is the central result of the paper.

\subsection{Engineering Consequences}

The following results are corollaries of the five-way equivalence, not independent contributions.

\textbf{Definition (Leverage):} $L(A) = |\mathrm{Capabilities}(A)| / \mathrm{DOF}(A)$.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{DOF--Reliability Isomorphism (Theorem~\ref{thm:dof-reliability}):} Architecture with $n$ DOF is isomorphic to series system with $n$ components. Each DOF is a failure point.
\item \textbf{Leverage--Error Tradeoff (Theorem~\ref{thm:leverage-error}):} Among architectures with equal capabilities, $L(A_1) > L(A_2) \implies P_{\mathrm{error}}(A_1) < P_{\mathrm{error}}(A_2)$.
\item \textbf{Modification Complexity Gap (Theorem~\ref{thm:leverage-gap}):} At fixed capabilities, expected modification cost is proportional to DOF.
\item \textbf{Physical Edit-Energy Floor (Theorem~\ref{thm:physical-energy-floor}):} Under Landauer calibration with explicit constant $j_\mathcal{M} > 0$, minimum edit-energy equals $j_\mathcal{M} \cdot \mathrm{DOF}(A)$.
\item \textbf{Optimal Architecture (Theorem~\ref{thm:optimal}):} $f(R) = \arg\max_{A:\,\mathrm{Cap}(A) \supseteq R} L(A)$ minimizes expected error and is computable.
\end{enumerate}

These constitute a formal proof that architectural optimization is decidable, with a provably optimal selection procedure. But their deeper content is that they all reduce to the DOF count---which the five-way equivalence explains from five independent first principles.

\subsection{Open Conjectures}

Two open problems remain within reach of the existing formalization machinery.

\textbf{Conjecture 1 (Unconditional Thermodynamic Selection).} Theorem~\ref{thm:thermodynamic-selection} assumes P~$\neq$~coNP to derive mandatory energy cost for DOF~$>1$ architectures. We conjecture the result holds unconditionally: thermodynamic cost scales directly with $\mathrm{srank}$, bypassing complexity theory. This would reduce the five-way equivalence to a chain of physical necessities with no complexity-theoretic hypothesis.

\textbf{Conjecture 2 (England Replication Bound).} Single-source architectures (DOF~$=1$) that generate derived instances are analogous to self-replicating systems in non-equilibrium thermodynamics. England's inequality~\cite{england2013statistical} bounds entropy production of such systems. We conjecture a formal analog: the entropy cost of maintaining consistency across $n$ replicated DOF strictly exceeds the entropy cost of a single DOF by a factor of at least $n$, with the bound tight at DOF~$=1$.

\textbf{Conjecture 3 (Wolpert Bound).} Wolpert and Kolchinsky's general thermodynamic bounds~\cite{wolpert2019stochastic} suggest cost scales with the number of relevant decision coordinates---i.e., with $\mathrm{srank}$---with an explicit constant derivable from Landauer calibration. We conjecture this gives a quantitative thermodynamic cost formula $\Omega(\mathrm{srank} \cdot k_B T \ln 2)$ per decision cycle, removing the existential witness and giving a closed-form bound.

\subsection{Dependency Chain}

This paper does not subsume Papers~1 and~2---it builds on them.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Paper~1} proves axis orthogonality $\to$ enables error independence (Theorem~\ref{thm:error-independence})
\item \textbf{Paper~2} proves DOF~$= 1$ guarantees SSOT coherence $\to$ establishes the second equivalence
\item \textbf{Paper~4} proves tractability boundary and thermodynamic lift $\to$ establishes the fourth and fifth equivalences
\item \textbf{This paper} closes the chain: leverage maximization (Framework~1) is equivalent to all of the above
\end{enumerate}

Paper~2 supplies the coherence constraint (DOF~$= 1$ per structural fact); this paper supplies the optimization rule inside the feasible set (maximize leverage) and proves that rule is selected by four additional independent frameworks.

\subsection{Scope}

Leverage characterizes the capability-to-DOF ratio. Performance, security, and other dimensions remain orthogonal concerns. Error independence is \emph{derived} from Paper~1's axis orthogonality theorem, not assumed. Physical edit-energy claims use a single explicit calibration constant ($j_\mathcal{M} > 0$) and no additional architectural axioms beyond the DOF--Reliability Isomorphism.
