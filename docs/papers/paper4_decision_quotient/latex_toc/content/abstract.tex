	extbf{Abstract}

We identify which coordinates of a factored state space suffice to determine optimal actions, and we quantify the algorithmic and physical costs of exploiting that structure. For a decision problem $\mathcal{D}=(A,S,U)$ with $S=X_1\times\cdots\times X_n$, a coordinate set $I$ is \emph{sufficient} when identical $I$-projections imply identical optimal actions. Our principal technical contributions are:

- Complexity landscape: deciding sufficiency and finding minimum sufficient sets are intractable in general (coNP- and $\Sigma_2^P$-hard depending on formulation); stochastic and sequential variants incur higher complexity (up to PP and PSPACE), while six structural subcases admit polynomial-time algorithms. Under ETH, succinct encodings imply $2^{\Omega(n)}$ lower bounds.

- Information-theoretic optimality: Bayesian updating uniquely minimizes expected log loss from first principles; the derivation requires only basic information inequalities (Gibbs' inequality) and explicit assumptions recorded in our machine-generated ledger.

- Thermodynamic and operational consequences: combining the bit-operation lower bounds with Landauer's principle ($k_B T\ln 2$ per irreversible bit erasure; proposed 1961; experimentally supported in 2012) yields explicit trade-offs between computational decision cost and thermodynamic work (energy per information change), making the physical cost of decision refinement quantitatively explicit.

Relationship to the series: this work builds on the prior formal concepts developed in the project—axis orthogonality (Paper 1) and Single Source of Truth / DOF = 1 (Paper 2)—and connects them to decision-theoretic sufficiency and computational/physical cost.

All core theorems are machine-checked in Lean~4 without \texttt{sorry} placeholders; complexity statements carry explicit hypotheses as Lean parameters and the assumption ledger accompanies the proofs. The paper presents constructive algorithms for tractable subcases and an explicit mutual-information argument for the side-information bounds.
