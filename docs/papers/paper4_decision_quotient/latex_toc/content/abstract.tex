We study physical information sufficiency as a decision-theoretic meta-problem. Given a decision problem $\mathcal{D}=(A,S,U)$ with factored state space $S=X_1\times\cdots\times X_n$, does a coordinate set $I$ determine the optimal action? Formally: $s_I=s'_I\implies\Opt(s)=\Opt(s')$. This asks whether projected information preserves optimal-action correspondence.

\textbf{Complexity landscape.} SUFFICIENCY-CHECK is \coNP-complete; MINIMUM-SUFFICIENT-SET is \coNP-complete; ANCHOR-SUFFICIENCY is $\Sigma_2^P$-complete. Under explicit-state encoding, polynomial-time algorithms hold for bounded actions, separable utility, low tensor rank, tree structure, bounded treewidth, and coordinate symmetry. Under succinct encoding with ETH, worst-case requires $2^{\Omega(n)}$ time. Under query access, $\Omega(|S|)$ lower bound.

\textbf{Bayesian reasoning.} The formalization includes two independent derivations. The standard path derives Bayesian updating from counting fractions through conditional probability to the decision quotient as a certainty measure. The thermodynamic path proves Bayesian updating is the unique update rule satisfying Landauer-compatible admissibility constraints: no free information, energy cost $\ge kT\ln2$ per bit, and integrity preservation.

\textbf{Physical grounding.} The paper formalizes a physical-to-core encoding $E:\mathcal{P}\to\mathcal{D}$ with transport rules: declared physical assumptions transfer to core assumptions, core claims lift to encoded physical instances. Discrete-time semantics (decision event = one tick) and thermodynamic lifts (bits to energy/carbon bounds) are formalized. Atoms with orbital transitions are agents (nonstationary decision circuits); ground-state atoms are passive.
