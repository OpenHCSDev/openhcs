\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Which coordinates of a system's state determine the optimal action? For a decision problem $\mathcal{D}=(A,S,U)$ with $S = X_1 \times \cdots \times X_n$, a coordinate set $I$ is sufficient when agreement on $I$ forces agreement on the optimal-action set:
\[
s_I = s'_I \implies \Opt(s) = \Opt(s').
\]
This is the basic problem of decision-relevant information: how much of the state must remain visible in order to preserve the decision boundary.

The question is easy to state but not algorithmically benign. Evaluating $U(a,s)$ on a given state is one task; certifying that an entire family of coordinates may be ignored is another. The latter carries universal quantification over state pairs and leads naturally to coNP and $\Sigma_2^P$ phenomena. The optimizer quotient packages the same issue structurally: it is the coarsest abstraction of the state space that preserves optimal-action distinctions.

This paper isolates that abstract problem. We work entirely with finite coordinate-structured decision problems and explicit encoding regimes.

Section~\ref{sec:encoding} fixes the computational model and the encoding regimes used by every complexity claim.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Shared foundations for coordinate sufficiency.} We formalize decision problems with coordinate structure, relevant-coordinate witnesses, exact relevance identification, and the optimizer quotient as the canonical decision-preserving abstraction.

\item \textbf{Exact complexity classifications.} We prove that \textsc{Sufficiency-Check} is coNP-complete, \textsc{Minimum-Sufficient-Set} is coNP-complete, and \textsc{Anchor-Sufficiency} is $\Sigma_2^P$-complete.

\item \textbf{Encoding-sensitive hardness and tractability.} We prove an explicit-state versus succinct dichotomy, derive ETH-conditioned lower bounds, and isolate natural tractable subcases under structural restrictions.

\item \textbf{Direct engineering corollaries.} We show that exact behavior-preserving minimization inherits the hardness of \textsc{Minimum-Sufficient-Set}, while conservative over-specification can be rational in the hard succinct regime under an explicit cost model.

\item \textbf{Machine-checked reductions and proof artifacts.} The main reductions and supporting lemmas are mechanized in Lean 4, yielding a reproducible proof artifact for the core complexity claims.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why the Problem Is Different}

The slogan of the paper is simple:
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Determining what you need to know can be harder than evaluating the full objective once everything is known.}
\end{quote}

The complexity gap comes from the structure of the question. Insufficiency has short witnesses: two states that agree on the proposed coordinates but induce different optimal-action sets. Sufficiency, by contrast, asks for the absence of every such witness. This difference drives the coNP classification and explains why relevance identification is not just another forward evaluation problem.
